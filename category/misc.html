How to Interview A Company

 Wed 01 January 2014 by Levi Gross

Many articles have been written about assessing interview candidates, however I have not seen many written on how interview candidates should assess the companies' and jobs they are interviewing for. So, I have compiled a list of things to look out for, this will (hopefully) help you get avoid working at a toxic location.

This is a list of things that I look out for when joining companies that I don't know about. You may not agree with everything listed here, or just some of the things. Please feel free to argue with everything listed here and write a counter list, I welcome it.

In the great quest for the perfect the job, there is no "one size fits all" solution. This list is by no means complete, so, please edit or add to the items listed here, I will do my best to update it, and attribute credit.

What to avoid

Avoid locations that are obsessed with a specific technology stack (unless you are as well).

Let's face it, technology stacks are a means to an end. Focusing on the technology instead of the solution is a recipe for disaster. Specific technology stacks are not guarantees for success and therefore should not be obsessed over.

Avoid locations with excessive technical debt.

This must be balanced with what is mentioned above. If the company allows their employees total freedom in adding to the technical stack, you should steer clear. A company's Bus Factor is very important. No one wants to spend a weekend learning some arcane system, because the person that maintained it has left the company and a new critical bug was just discovered.

Avoid locations where the people interviewing look overworked

If the employees look overworked, if you join, you will be overworked as well.

Avoid locations that will not be able to tell you what your initial workload will look like

When you join a company, the company should have a somewhat accurate estimate of what your initial workload should look like (at least for the first 3-6 months). Avoid places that don't.

Avoid locations that seriously affect your quality of life

Your quality of life is important, and you should be assertive in ensuring that your new job won't have a detrimental affect on it. I guarantee you a company doesn't care how many all nighters you pull to get the job done. All they see is how to efficiently make use of their human capital and will squeeze you like a sponge as long as you are worth anything to them. Your job requires your full mental capacity, which requires that you maintain a certain quality of life.

If you are offered a higher salary in exchange for less equity, take the higher salary.

Equity rarely amounts to anything significant. Many times it is used to pacify overworked employees. Take the extra cash today.

Avoid locations that do not have an official ramp-up process

Every employee, no matter how senior, must learn how things work at every company they join. Your initial days will require a lot of "hand holding" and help; this is normal. Avoid places that throw you into the lions den with a dull knife.

Avoid locations that have even one brogrammer

Remember, even one bad employee can ruin the environment for everyone.

Avoid locations with immature and childish employees

You want to work in a place where people are respectful and mature. Otherwise, office drama will play a serious part of your work day.

Avoid locations with conceited people

No one wants to be around conceited people, let alone work with them.

Avoid locations that shun diversity

You want to work at places that embrace diversity. Remember, the reason the USA is one of the best places on earth, is that we embrace diversity.

Avoid places that don't respect you or your property

This can be interpreted in many ways and I will leave this up to the reader's intuition. However, I will provide one example. If your company expects you to use your cellphone as a part of your job, they should at least pay some if not your entire cellphone bill (in addition to your current salary) or provide you with a company cellphone.

Avoid locations with that lack a systematic processes

Processes and policies allow companies to scale. A lack of some form of standardization will lead to all sorts of technical and logistical debt.

Avoid locations with no office culture

Every company, big and small should have some form of office culture. You want to work in a friendly environments and be on good terms with your co-workers. Many of these relationships can be forged at events promoted by a company's culture. A company lacking culture, lacks harmony.

Avoid locations that have unwelcome surprises during the interview

If you experience an unwelcome surprise during the interview, imagine how many "surprises" you will experience during your employment.

Avoid locations that ask useless interview questions

If you are asked questions that are not relevant to the job you are interviewing for, decline to answer them. Here is an incomplete list of useless interview questions.

  1. How many traffic lights are there in New York City?
  2. How do you reverse a number without coercing it into a string?
  3. How do you traverse an array without using loops?

Avoid locations that ask you to contribute to their code base during the interview process

You are being interviewed and have not been hired. You should not be working on the company's code base during your interview.

Avoid locations that ask you to take a large amount of time off of work to interview without compensating you

A company should respect the time that the interview candidates take to come in to interview. If they ask you to take a day or two off to interview, they should compensate you monetarily.

Avoid locations that do not have a code of ethics

A code of ethics is very important and every company should possess one. Avoid places that don't.

Avoid locations with an unlimited paid vacation policy

This is a nice way of saying "The company vacation policy is subject to our discretion on a case by case basis". Knowing how much paid vacation you possess allows you to plan your vacation and not have to worry if you're asking for too much.

Avoid locations with questionable business practices

If the company you are interviewing by may be breaking the law or doing something you deem unethical; walk away.

Avoid locations with individual ownership requirements

All new and current systems should be owned by the engineering team. You don't want to be the only one on call for everything you build. It leads to a culture where people are afraid to innovate in fear of losing their nights and weekends.

Avoid locations that force you to use your personal social media accounts for company advertisements

It is fine for a company to ask you to post company announcements or job listings on your social media accounts. It is not OK for them to force you.

Avoid locations with a high turn over rate

If a company cannot retain employees, there is a good reason for it; walk away.

Avoid locations that lack a merit based monetary raises and bonus's

If a company doesn't show appreciation for the hard work their employees put in, they don't deserve the employees working for them and you shouldn't join. And no, mugs and tee-shirts aren't considered a merit based monetary raises and bonus's!

Questions to ask during your interview

Obtain very specific information about the companies benefits

Many companies offer benefit packages, make sure you get a very clear picture of what is included, in particular the health, dental and vision benefits. A better benefits package can sometimes trump a higher salary. Here is an incomplete list of questions you should ask about a company's benefit package:

  1. Which insurance company provides the medical, dental, vision plans?
  2. What are the plan names?
  3. Can you please send me a copy of the medical, dental, and vision plans?
  4. Can you please send me a complete list of all of the benefits provided?
  5. How long after joining the company do the medical, dental, and vision start?
  6. Are there any limits or caveats that I should know about?

Obtain very specific information about the companies tech stack

Reverse interview the employees about the work they do, the technology they use. Don't be afraid to get as nuanced as possible. This will show you at least 3 things:

  1. How well the current employees understand the stack that they are using.
  2. The technical debt that you will be dealing with (should you decide to join).
  3. If they make sane technical decisions.

Ask the employees to tell you something they don't like about their current job

This is very important. Familiarity breeds contempt, and how employees answer this question will tell you a lot about the company.

Look at GlassDoor.com before you go in for an interview

GlassDoor can give you some insight into company internals, make use of it.

Reach out to former employees, ask them about their experience

Be polite and don't be pushy. Let them know you are considering joining company XYZ and you would like to hear about their experiences at XYZ company. Their insight can provide invaluable information on whether or not you should join XYZ company.

Identify a list of reasons you would like to join the company and validate those reasons

Say you want to join XYZ Inc because they use Python. During the interview ask how and when they use Python. Ensure that the company's use of Python is what you are looking for. Knowing what you are getting into is very important.

If the company has public code on GitHub, look it over

Every company has their own engineering process. When a company publishes code, they are usually proud of it. This is one of the best ways to gauge the engineering process within a company.

Find something technical to criticize and politely mention it during the interview

Dealing with criticism is an important part of every job, especially technical jobs. Being mature when accepting both valid and invalid criticism is very important.

Additionally, you should gauge the response. If you don't like the response or the way that they deal with the critique; walk away.

If you interview with the CEO of the company; question that as if you were looking to invest in the company

When you work for a company, you're throwing your lot with the company's lot. Make sure the company is making sound business decisions . Here is an incomplete list of questions you should ask a the company's CEO.

  1. What do you feel are the biggest challenges the business faces within the next 6 months?
  2. What is your plan to overcome those challenges?
  3. What were the biggest challenges that the business has overcome in the past 6 months?

Speak with the employees of the company at meetups

You may learn things that are not mentioned during the interview, you may meet an employee that you did not interview with. This can also help you gain insight into other parts of the company.

Try to understand the companies recent history

If the company just underwent some big changes, find out what was changed, why it was changed, how it was changed, and how it will and does affect the company's or team's process/performance.

Obtain a clear list of expectations for the job that you are interviewing for

Gauge whether you feel that expectations are excessive. It will also help you negotiate a higher salary should they be excessive.
